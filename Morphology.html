Morphology

It should be noted that it is unclear in many cases whether we are dealing with affixation processes within a word, or syntactically dependent words / clitics, like English 'up' of 'look up (the answer)'. This is especially an issue for verbs.

Nouns

There are three main aspects of noun morphology: gender, number & definite marking; diminutives; post-posing marking.

Gender, Number and Definiteness
Singular nouns may be marked for masculine or feminine gender, where -m- marks the masculine and -shi- marks the feminine. Ordinarily, nouns in citation do not have a gender marker. However, the definiteness marker -a is possible in citation forms (though is not generally used) except with the feminine plural, and when present, the noun will have a number-gender marker. The interpretation of -a is uncertain and requires further research: it may mark definiteness or focus, but the meaning 'definiteness' is tenatively selected based on the kinds of contexts where it is most often used (discussed elsewhere).

zhii     'tree'     zhii-m-a      'the tree'
xam     'lion     xam-m-a     'the (m) lion'
xam-sh-a         'the (f) lion'

There are three plural suffixes, -ku- for masculine (m), -zi for feminine (f) and -na for mixed gender (c). Gender appears to be semantically determined, and for animates, corresponds to the male / female distinction.

The unmarked (discourse-neutral) gender for singulars seems to be masculine, and the default plural is feminine. Selecting the marked gender (feminine singular or masculine plural) either indicates specific sex-identification for animates (i.e. "female dog" as opposed to "dog"; "male dogs" as opposed to "dogs"), or something special 'special' about an inanimate noun: the semantics of the marked form need more investigation.

Note that the definite marker -a does not appear in the feminine plural, so  abazi and not *abaza. There is no common-plural with just -n (*aban, *zhiin). Citation words cannot have just the stem plus gender, this *abam, *abaku, *abashi, but in phrasal contexts such forms exist (see below).

Examples of marked-gender semantics are:

chuinn-shi am-shi         'small knife (f), especially blunt'
chuinn-m am-m-a         'small knife (m)'
kao-ku /'ere-kwa          'long ropes (m), especially strong'
kao-zi /'ere-zi                'long ropes (f)'
g//oo-ku /'ui-kwa          'big stones (m), few but very big in size'
g//oo-zi /'ui-zi               'big stones (f)'

In phrases, prenominal adjectives tend to agree in number and gender with a gender-marked noun (see the section on NP syntax).

/oann                           'child'
tui /oann                      'beautiful child'
tui-shi /oann-sh-a        'beautiful female child'
tui-m /oann-m-a          'beautiful male child'
g//oo aba                      'big dog'
g//oo-m aba-m-a           'big male dog'
g//oo-shi aba-sh-a         'big female dog'
kao-ku /'ere-kwa           'long ropes (m)'
hunn-zi aba-zi               'those dogs'
hunn-ku aba-kw-a         'those male dogs'
hunn-zi g//oo-zi aba-zi   'those big dogs'

Gender without definiteness is also possible in possessor phrases, for example:


    kakhwe-m  am                'the man's knife'
    (man-m.s knife)
    kakhwe-ku  am                'the men's knife'
    (men-m.pl knife)

There is no agreement for common gender (where masc. and fem. features conflict):

hunn xam-na            'those lions (c)'"
*hunn-zi xam-na      'those lions (c)'"
*hunn-ku xam-na     'those lions (c)'

We also have noted one irregular plural: the noun /oann 'child' has the plural /oare 'children'. This plural marker can combine with the regular plural markers, e.g. /oarena 'children (c)', /oarekwa 'children (m)', /oarezi 'children (f or unmarked)'.

DiminutivesDiminutives are formed with the suffix -/wa (see /oann "child"), which precedes number-gender-definite marking:

bie               'zebra'
bie/wa         'little zebra'
bie/wana     'little zebras (c)'
bie/wazi      'little zebras'
aba/wazi     'little dogs'
aba/waku     'little dogs (m)'
aba/wakwa  'the little dogs (m)'

There are also lexicalized diminutives:

mbuta/wa     'hyrax"       mbuta 'rabbit
tshau/wa      'finger'         tshau 'hand'

The diminutive is not an agreement-triggering category:

    haann-shi tuinn-shi dam   -/wa-sh-a        'that beautiful rabbit (dim)'
     that-f.s.   beaut.-f   rabbit-dim-fem-def

    *haann-/wa dam-/wa
    *haann-/wa-m dam-/wa-m-a

Postposing
Modifiers moved to the right of the noun are marked with the definite marker -a, in definite NPs, or with -e, in indefinite NPs.

    hiin-ku aba-kw-a            'these dogs (m)'
    aba-kw-a hiin-kw-a        'these dogs (m)'
    kao-m /'ere-m-a              '(the) long rope'
    /'ere-m-a kao-m-a           '(the) long rope'
    kao /'ere                          'long rope'
    /'ere kao-e                       'long rope'
    g//oo !aba /'ui                  'big flat rock'
    /'ui g/oo-e !aba-e             'big flat rock'


Verbs

Verb inflectional morphology is limited to three suffixes. The past tense marker is -aha, and follows all derivational suffixes.

iunn        'eat'          iuaha        'ate'
g/ii          'sing'        g/iaha       'sang'
muunn    'see'          muaha      'saw'
tshaa       'be tired'    tshaaha    'was tired'

There is a little-understood clause / mood marker -a found in clauses like:

    Chirambam iu-a hina    'Chiramba will have eaten'

An equally under-understood TAM affix is -inn:

    ema //oinn        'he slept'
cf. ema //oeha      'he slept'

The benefactive suffix is -ma-, which requires an additional n before the past suffix:

uu    'send'        u-a-ma-na-ha    'sent to'
/oa   'kill'         /oa-ma-n-aha    'killed for'     ke /oa-ma    'will kill'

The passive is -we-.

iuweha        'it was eaten'
muweha      'it was seen'

The reflexive is -si.

ema ke muunn-si            'he sees himself'
ema muunn-si-aha          'he saw himself'

The dative and causative are -(k)axu.

    ta ke hina aba /he gam-axu              'I will throw at the dog'
    (gam 'throw')
    ta /'uizi a gam-axu-aha abam /he    'I threw rocks at the dog'

    iunn-kaxu-aha                    'fed'
    g//ai-kaxu                            '(will) make run'
    n/waa-kaxu                         '(will) make cook'

Adverbs

Adverbs can be formed from predicates via the suffix -se.

tui        'beautiful'     tuise        'well'
/'aann   'be wet'        /'aannse   'while wet'
g//oo     'big'             g/oose     'very much'
//uum    'short'          //uumse   'shortly, not much'
shubu   'quick'          shubuse   'quickly'
